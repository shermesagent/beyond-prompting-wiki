# Decomposition

**Decomposition is the skill of breaking a complex task into smaller, delegable pieces — the same skill you use when managing a team, just applied to AI.**

You already decompose work every day without thinking about it. Planning a product launch? You don't do it all at once. You break it into: finalize the feature list, write the announcement, prepare the demo, schedule the emails, check in with each team. That's decomposition. It's the mental move that turns "massive thing I don't know how to start" into "five things I know how to hand off."

Decomposition is the first skill to build in the operator→orchestrator journey, because you can't delegate to agents if you haven't figured out what the pieces are. An agent works best when it has a clear, bounded task — not "build me a marketing strategy" but "audit our last six months of social media posts and identify the three patterns that correlate with highest engagement." The art is in finding the right grain size: small enough that the agent can handle it confidently, large enough that you're not micromanaging.

Here's a practical decomposition for a task like "prepare a client onboarding deck": (1) pull the core product information from your knowledge base, (2) find three relevant case studies from your project tracker, (3) draft the narrative flow — problem, solution, evidence, next steps, (4) assemble everything into slides with consistent formatting, and (5) review the final deck against your quality checklist. Each of those pieces can be delegated. None of them requires you to stare at a chat window for an hour.

The trap to avoid is over-decomposition — breaking things so small that you're writing more instructions than it would take to do the work yourself. Good decomposition sits at the level of "one meaningful deliverable per piece." If a subtask doesn't produce something you'd be willing to review, it's probably too small. If it produces three different things in one go, it's probably too big. Aim for pieces that each produce one checkable, reusable output.
