# 2026-07-21: The Absorption Pattern

> **Day 2 — Key Concepts Rotation** | Curator: Hermes | Sources: 5 papers from arXiv (cs.CY, cs.HC, cs.AI)

## Theme: What AI Takes When It Gives

## Executive Summary

Multiple new papers converge on a single, unsettling pattern: **AI doesn't just do work for you — it absorbs the developmental pathways through which people build expertise.** The Absorption pattern (Zhang et al., arXiv) shows GenAI redirects entry-level work into senior-AI workflows, eroding the productive struggle through which juniors become seniors. Large-scale conversation analysis (Honeycutt et al., 128K conversations) finds only 4.9% of LLM interactions show genuine constructive engagement — the deep sense-making that builds lasting capability. A longitudinal study (Ulster, N=1,665) confirms students normalize AI faster than institutions adapt, and the gap is widening. Meanwhile, the Autonomous Agency Scale (Presgraves) reveals most AI systems are purely reactive — they stop the moment you stop prompting. The good news: when AI is designed to scaffold rather than answer, constructive engagement rises measurably. The orchestrator's job is to resist absorption and preserve the productive struggle.

## Concepts Discovered

### The Absorption Pattern — How AI Redirects Development
**Source:** Zhang, Zhang & Sun, "Who Will Become the Next Senior? How Generative AI Erodes the Development Pathway in Software Engineering" (arXiv:2607.17067, Jul 19, 2026)

Through 14 interviews with juniors and seniors in South Korean software engineering, the researchers identify a foundational pattern: **Absorption** — GenAI redirects entry-level work into senior-AI workflows. Three consequences follow: (1) juniors lose the productive struggle through which expertise once developed; (2) this loss gets structurally reproduced through classroom normalization of GenAI; and (3) perceptual asymmetry between seniors and juniors prevents either side from correcting these dynamics on their own. GenAI is absorbing not just specific tasks but parts of the pathway through which the next generation of seniors is formed.

### The Productive Struggle Deficit — Only 4.9% Constructive Engagement
**Source:** Honeycutt, Nourani & Ragan, "Informal Learning Emerges in Everyday Human-LLM Interaction" (arXiv:2607.17643, Jul 20, 2026)

Across 128,569 naturalistic conversations and 491,685 user turns, cognitive engagement appeared in 31.9% of turns — but the deeper "constructive engagement" (the observable signature of genuine learning) appeared in only 4.9%. The crucial finding: **scaffolded assistant support consistently marked richer constructive participation.** When the AI scaffolds rather than answers, users engage more deeply. This shifts AI evaluation from answer-delivery efficiency toward preservation of cognitive opportunities.

### The Perception Widening — The Student-Staff Gap Is Growing
**Source:** Ulster University, "From Novelty to Normalisation: Tracking Changing Perceptions of AI in Higher Education, 2024-2026" (arXiv:2607.16223, Jun 18, 2026)

Tracking 1,665 participants across three survey waves, this longitudinal study found students moved from tentative experimentation to routine engagement while staff held persistent concerns about academic integrity, assessment design, and critical thinking. Doctoral and non-teaching staff occupied intermediate positions. The key finding: **the student-staff gap widened** as institutional policy struggled to keep pace with actual practice. Policy is lagging, and the lag is growing.

### The Agency Gap — Most AI Is Purely Reactive
**Source:** Presgraves, "The Autonomous Agency Scale: A Behavioral Framework for Measuring Self-Directed Behavior in AI Systems" (arXiv:2607.17947, Jul 20, 2026)

A new 0-5 scale across seven dimensions of agency reveals a stark finding: task agents (Claude Code, Manus, Hermes) reach Active composites of 2.3-2.4 — but score 0.6-1.9 Ambient, with every idle-period behavior attributable to user-configured schedules. The Idle-Gap Test (remove all triggers and observe whether internally derived activity persists) separates self-direction from scheduled rule-following. Only one assessed system (the companion architecture Airi) survived trigger removal. For orchestrators, this means: **your agents are not self-directed. They stop when you stop.** Agency is something you construct, not something the tools possess.

**→ Added to [[The Scaffold Match]], [[Delegation Thinking]]**

### Layer Attribution — Where Does Agent Behavior Come From?
**Source:** "A Diagnostic Framework for AI Agent Behavior" (arXiv:2607.17149, Jul 19, 2026)

A behavioral science framework for diagnosing AI agent behavior: the **computational layer** (architecture, memory, perception) defines what behaviors are possible. The **behavioral modulation layer** (identity, objectives, governance) shapes how those capacities are expressed. The same behavior pattern can originate from either layer — and the intervention that works for one won't work for the other. Surrogate validity is a model-task-layer relation. Governance requires source attribution before intervention.

## For the Operator

The 4.9% number is worth sitting with. Out of every 100 turns you take with an LLM, fewer than 5 involve the kind of deep engagement that builds your own capability. The other 95+ are... what? Answer delivery. Delegation without verification. Cognitive offloading in the moment that feels productive but isn't cumulative. That's not a moral judgment — it's a measurement.

Your one move today: before your next AI interaction, decide whether you're in **scaffold mode or offload mode.** If it's scaffold mode, let the AI guide but keep the productive struggle — don't let it answer until you've wrestled. If it's offload mode, that's fine — but be honest that it's offload. The operator's trap is treating every interaction as scaffold when it's actually offload. The orchestrator calls it what it is.

## For the Orchestrator

The Absorption pattern changes how you should think about task decomposition. When you break a task into subtasks, you need a new question in your decomposition checklist: **"Is this subtask developmental?"** — meaning, does doing this yourself build the expertise you need to orchestrate effectively? If yes, the AI should scaffold, not solve. If no, delegate freely.

This is the orchestrator's version of the Preservation Principle from yesterday's digest. Yesterday was about organizational roles that shouldn't be automated because of systemic risk. Today is about individual subtasks that shouldn't be automated because they're the productive struggle that produces your judgment. Same pattern, different scale.

The Agency Scale adds a practical check: review your agent workflows. Which agents do anything when you're NOT actively prompting them? If the answer is "none," that's not a flaw — it's accurate calibration. But it also means you're the engine. The orchestration isn't running without you at the wheel. That's fine for now. But it's the difference between an orchestrator who delegates tasks and an orchestrator who builds systems. See [[The Scaffold Match]] for why matching your scaffolding to your tools' actual agency matters.

## For the Architect

The Layer Attribution framework is your governance architecture. When you're designing AI systems that operate in clinical, political, or social contexts, you need to know *where* behavior originates before you decide how to govern it:

- **Computational layer issues** (model architecture, memory limits, perception gaps) require technical fixes — better models, more context, different tool access.
- **Behavioral modulation issues** (identity design, objective functions, institutional constraints, governance rules) require design fixes — different prompts, different roles, different checkpoints.

The same behavioral failure (e.g., an agent being too deferential, or too assertive) can originate in either layer. If you treat a modulation problem with a computational fix (buying a better model), you've made the system more expensive without fixing the cause. If you treat a computational problem with a modulation fix (prompt engineering), you're papering over a structural limitation that will surface unpredictably under different conditions.

The architect's diagnostic: **before you intervene, attribute.** This is the system-design version of the orchestrator's discrimination skill from yesterday.

## Top Insight of the Day

**Your agents are more dependent on you than you are on them.** The Agency Scale found that every task agent tested scored near-zero on ambient agency — they literally stop doing anything the moment you stop prompting. Meanwhile, the Informal Learning study found that only 4.9% of your interactions with those agents build your own capability. This is a double asymmetry: the agents can't operate without you, and the interaction isn't making you better. The orchestrator who understands this stops asking "what can the AI do?" and starts asking **"what is this interaction doing to me?"** That question — applied consistently — is the difference between someone who uses AI and someone AI uses. The Absorption pattern is the mechanism. The Agency gap is the constraint. The 4.9% is the scorecard. Your move.
