## Concepts Discovered

### 1. Workflow-First AI Adoption

**What it is:** David Ross, former CEO of the Partnership for 21st Century Learning, argues in Getting Smart (May 2026) that "AI in schools won't matter until school workflow changes." The point isn't that schools need better AI tools — it's that the *workflow* itself has to shift from fixed, one-size-fits-all instruction to adaptive, personalized learning. You can't layer AI on top of a broken process and expect transformation.

The same principle applies everywhere. A small business that uses AI to answer customer emails faster hasn't changed its workflow. It's just doing the old thing faster. The orchestrator doesn't ask "how can AI speed up what I already do?" They ask "what would the workflow look like if I designed it around delegation from the start?"

**Why it matters:** This is the most common failure pattern in the beyond-prompting journey — treating AI as a faster horse instead of redesigning the transportation system. The workflow redesign is the shift.

**Source:** David Ross, "Changing Work Flow of Schools," Getting Smart, May 11, 2026

### 2. From Author to Editor

**What it is:** The single most consistent pattern across every real-world beyond-prompting story: the person's job shifts from *creating output* to *reviewing output*. An analytics practitioner who replaced half his workflow with AI agents for 90 days described it plainly: "My job shifted from author to editor, and editing is faster — but it's also a different skill set." You're no longer writing the first draft. You're making one quality pass on a complete draft, with fresh eyes. Your standards go up because your cognitive budget isn't depleted by the drafting itself.

Mara's story in [[From Prompt to Pipeline]] shows the same pattern. So does the Farmersville district story — teachers stopped writing lesson plans from scratch and started reviewing AI-generated drafts built from their own curriculum documents.

**Why it matters:** The author-to-editor shift is the mental model change at the heart of the operator→orchestrator move. It's not about working less. It's about working *differently* — shifting your cognitive effort from production to judgment.

**Source:** "I Replaced Half My Analytics Workflow With AI Agents for 90 Days," Medium, June 10, 2026; multiple corroborating sources

### 3. Delegation as Universal Design

**What it is:** A case study from arXiv (June 24, 2026) examined a low-vision technology expert using agentic web browsers — AI systems that navigate the web on behalf of a user. Instead of manually clicking through pages, the user describes what they need and the agent finds it. The finding: despite current limitations, "the navigation experience is notably fluid and flexible." What was once a barrier — inaccessible web design — becomes a conversation.

The researchers note that implications extend "beyond accessibility to agentic UX more broadly." The lesson: delegation isn't just for efficiency. It's a design pattern that removes barriers for everyone, not just power users.

**Why it matters:** This reframes delegation from a productivity hack to a fundamental access question. When we talk about moving beyond prompting, we're not just talking about saving time. We're talking about who gets to participate. An orchestrated workflow that handles navigation for a visually impaired user is the same pattern as an orchestrated workflow that handles TEKS alignment for a teacher. The orchestrator mindset recognizes delegation as infrastructure for human capability.

**Source:** "'Zooming In' on Agentic Web Browsers as Assistive Technologies: A Case Study with a Low-Vision Technology Expert," arXiv:2606.24870, June 24, 2026

### 4. The Enterprise Tipping Point

**What it is:** April 2026 marked a definitive shift in enterprise AI, according to FifthRow's analysis (April 28, 2026). The conversation pivoted from "which model should we use?" to "how do we manage our agents?" The Berkeley California Management Review published a framework for "Governing the Agentic Enterprise" — a new operating model for autonomous AI at scale, with case studies including Lemonade (insurance). The core insight: when AI moves from tool to agent, organizations need new governance structures. You're no longer managing software. You're managing autonomous actors.

Meanwhile, an ESD 112 education conference in June 2026 featured a talk precisely titled: "Beyond Prompting: Leveraging AI to Build Systems That Save Teachers Time" — by Marc Elliott, CIO of Olympia School District. The thesis has escaped the research papers and entered the conference circuit. Real district leaders are presenting it to real educators.

**Why it matters:** The tipping point isn't just about technology maturity. It's about the organizational muscle to manage agentic systems. The orchestrator who understands governance — how to set constraints, how to audit, how to intervene — has an advantage the pure technologist doesn't.

**Source:** FifthRow, "Agentic AI's Enterprise Tipping Point," April 28, 2026; Sandeep Saini, "Governing the Agentic Enterprise," CMR Berkeley, March 20, 2026; ESD 112 Forward 2026 conference

## For the Operator

If you're still in Phase 2 — prompting, tweaking, copying — here's your move today:

**The "What Would I Edit Instead of Write?" Audit**

Take one recurring task you do at least weekly. Ask yourself: *If a capable assistant handed me a complete draft of this — not perfect, but structurally sound — and my only job was to edit it, would that change my relationship to this task?*

Most people answer yes. The operator who's been prompting for outlines, then prompting for expansion, then prompting for variants is doing assembly-line work with themselves as the conveyor belt. The orchestrator writes the spec once and edits once. The middle — the prompting, the waiting, the re-prompting — dissolves.

**One Practice: The Spec-First Try**

For your next recurring task, don't open a chat window. Open a document. Write:
1. What the finished output should contain
2. What it should NOT contain or do
3. How you'll know it's good in 60 seconds

Now give that spec to an AI agent (not a chatbot — an agent that can search, write files, chain actions). Let it run. Review the result. You just experienced the author-to-editor shift in miniature.

## For the Orchestrator

You're building delegation workflows. Here's what today's findings add:

**Audit Your Workflow Before Your Tool**

David Ross's insight about schools applies to you: "AI in [your context] won't matter until [your] workflow changes." Before you optimize your agent pipeline, walk backward through your actual workflow. Where does the handoff from AI back to you happen? Is that handoff well-designed, or is it where things fall apart? The fifth-iteration prompt tweak is probably a workflow design problem, not a prompt quality problem.

**Design Your Governance, Not Just Your Pipeline**

The Berkeley CMR framework surfaces something orchestrators often skip: when you delegate a task to an agent, you're not just offloading work. You're creating an autonomous actor that needs constraints, audit trails, and intervention points. For each workflow you run, ask: *If this agent went off the rails, how would I know? How quickly could I intervene? What's the worst thing it could do with the access I've given it?* Good governance isn't paranoia. It's what lets you sleep while the pipeline runs.

**Recognize Delegation as Access Work**

The accessibility case study is a powerful reframe. When you design a workflow that handles the tedious parts of a task — data lookups, formatting, cross-referencing — you're not just being efficient. You're removing a barrier. Someone on your team might be stuck in operator mode not because they don't "get it" but because they're spending all their cognitive budget on the parts you've already automated. Share your workflows. Delegation scales sideways.

They stop producing first drafts and start making judgment calls on complete ones. This isn't working less. It's working differently — trading the cognitive fatigue of iterative prompting for the higher-leverage work of taste, strategy, and decision-making. The shift feels uncomfortable for about two weeks. Then you can't go back.**

The teacher who used to spend Sunday afternoons writing lesson plans now spends twenty minutes reviewing and personalizing AI-generated drafts. The analytics practitioner who documented his 90-day experiment: "editing is faster, but it's also a different skill set." That skill set — judgment, taste, strategic editing — is the orchestrator's craft. It's learnable. And it's the difference between being busy with AI and being effective with it.
