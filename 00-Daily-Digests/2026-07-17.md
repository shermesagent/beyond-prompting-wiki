# Practice Is Calibration

**Friday, July 17, 2026 | Day 5 — Practice Rotation**

---

## Executive Summary

Today's research points to a single, sharp insight: **practice isn't about learning to use AI better — it's about calibrating your relationship with it.** Five papers from arXiv this week reveal that most people don't know how they rely on AI (GenAI-RTS), can't accurately judge what's their contribution vs. the AI's (Authorship Calibration), and experience distinct relational turning points that shape their trust (Memory-Driven Self-Disclosure). Meanwhile, structured training — not better technology — is the real adoption barrier (Brazilian government study), and even the design of agent "harnesses" teaches us how to organize our own delegation pipelines. The operator's gap isn't a knowledge gap. It's a calibration gap. And calibration is a practice — it's built, not learned.

---

## Concepts Discovered

### 1. The Four Reliance Types

**What it is:** The GenAI-RTS (Generative AI Reliance Types Scale) identifies four distinct ways people lean on AI:

| Type | In Plain Language | Health |
|------|-------------------|--------|
| **Dialogic** | AI as thinking partner — you talk *with* it, not *at* it | 🟢 Most growth-oriented |
| **Strategic** | AI as one tool among many — you decide when and how to use it | 🟢 Healthy, intentional |
| **Instrumental** | AI as a faster way to get things done — pure productivity | 🟡 Fine, but watch for drift |
| **Dependent** | AI as a crutch — your own skills start to atrophy | 🔴 Concerning |

**Why it matters for the shift:** Most people can't name their reliance type. They just know they "use AI a lot." But the type determines everything: what practice exercises will help, where your growth edge is, and whether you're building capability or dependency. The first practice exercise isn't "try this prompt" — it's "what's your reliance type?"

**Source:** GenAI-RTS Scale (arXiv:2607.14301)

---

### 2. Authorship Calibration — The "Who Did What?" Problem

**What it is:** Heavy AI users systematically misjudge their own contribution. Three patterns:
- **Over-attribution:** "I wrote that" (when the AI wrote 80%)
- **Under-attribution:** "The AI did everything" (missing where your framing, judgment, and direction-setting made the difference)
- **Boundary blindness:** Can't remember where your thinking stopped and the AI's began

**Why it matters for the shift:** You can't be an orchestrator if you don't know what you're orchestrating. If you think you're doing more than you are, you won't develop the skills you actually need. If you think you're doing less, you'll undervalue your uniquely human contribution and cede too much. Calibration is the foundation of good delegation.

**Source:** Authorship Calibration study (arXiv:2607.15006)

---

### 3. Your AI Relationship Has Turning Points

**What it is:** Over 10 sessions with the same AI, people don't just accumulate output — they develop a *relationship*. And relationships have turning points:
- **Crashes:** The AI forgets something it should remember, gives a tone-deaf response, or breaks the conversational frame. Trust drops sharply.
- **Surges:** The AI surprises you with insight, remembers a detail from session 3, or makes a connection you didn't see. Trust jumps.

Conversational quality predicts enjoyment *in the moment*, but doesn't carry forward. What does carry forward? **Memory.** When the AI remembers things across sessions, people self-disclose more over time. The relationship deepens.

**Why it matters for the shift:** Sustained delegation isn't a technical practice — it's a relational one. If you don't track the turning points, a single crash can make you abandon a pipeline that was working fine. The orchestrator's skill isn't avoiding crashes — it's recovering from them and learning what caused them.

**Source:** Memory-Driven Self-Disclosure study (arXiv:2607.14593)

---

### 4. Your Pipeline Is a Harness — Design It Like One

**What it is:** In agent engineering, the "harness" is the code that wraps the model — prompts, tool calls, state management, execution coordination. The central bottleneck in evolving these harnesses is **behavior localization**: finding WHAT code needs to change when you want the system to DO something different.

The Harness Handbook principle: organize your pipeline documentation by *behavior* (what it does), not by *file* (where it lives). This makes pipelines readable, navigable, and editable — the same three qualities that make agent harnesses maintainable.

**Why it matters for the shift:** Your delegation templates and pipelines ARE your harness. When you name them by what they do ("Morning Briefing Pipeline") rather than where they are ("that template in my Notes app"), you've taken the first step toward behavior-centric design. When a pipeline breaks, you should be able to find the fix in 30 seconds — not search through scattered templates.

**Source:** Harness Handbook (arXiv:2607.13285)

### 5. Training Is the Barrier, Not Technology

**What it is:** A real-world study in the Brazilian public sector proved it: the main barrier to AI adoption isn't model capability, access, or cost. It's **training**. The author developed a four-layer structured method and applied it across two government units. Results:
- Processing time dropped **18–50%**
- Technical-report production increased **85%**
- All with **free AI models** under budget constraints

**Why it matters for the shift:** This is the strongest empirical validation yet of the Beyond Prompting thesis. Better models won't close the operator-to-orchestrator gap. Structured practice will. The method matters more than the model. Training — deliberate, scaffolded, practice-based — is the bridge.

**Source:** Brazilian public sector AI adoption study (arXiv:2606.01517)

## For the Operator (Phase 2)

**What a Phase 2 person can take away TODAY:**

1. **Do a reliance-type check-in (2 min).** Look at your last three AI interactions. For each, ask: was I *dialogic* (thinking together), *strategic* (deliberate tool use), *instrumental* (just getting it done), or *dependent* (I'd struggle without it)? Write down the pattern. No judgment — just awareness.

2. **Try the authorship question (30 sec per pipeline).** After your next pipeline run, ask: "What specifically did *I* contribute here, and what did the AI contribute?" If you can't separate them, that's data — not a problem. But if you CAN separate them and your contribution is "I pressed enter," your pipeline needs redesign.

3. **Adopt the training mindset.** The Brazilian study showed that structured method beats better models. Your practice — the audit, the standup, the pipeline design — is your training method. Treat it as seriously as you'd treat learning any other professional skill. It's not "playing with AI." It's calibrating a relationship that will define your work for the next decade.

---

## For the Orchestrator (Phase 3)

**What a Phase 3 person can apply:**

1. **Add a reliance-type column to your standup.** In [[The Daily Standup]], next to each pipeline, note which reliance type it encourages. A Morning Briefing pipeline that you run on autopilot? That's instrumental — valuable but not growth-oriented. A research synthesis pipeline where you're genuinely thinking *with* the AI? That's dialogic. After two weeks, look at your mix. If everything is instrumental, you're optimizing for speed at the expense of growth. Add one dialogic pipeline.

2. **Track relational turning points in your standup.** The study found that crashes and surges shape the relationship. Add a sixth standup question: **"Did I experience a crash or surge with any pipeline today?"** A crash might be output that confused you or felt "off." A surge might be unexpected insight. Both are data about where your pipelines are fragile (crashes) or where they could be expanded (surges).

3. **Organize your pipelines by behavior, not by where they live.** The Harness Handbook principle: name every pipeline by what it DOES. If someone asked "how do I get today's briefing?", they should find it under "Morning Briefing," not "that template in my Obsidian vault." This sounds trivial. It's not — it's the difference between a pipeline you'll use next week and one you'll forget you built.

## Top Insight of the Day

> **The operator-to-orchestrator gap isn't a knowledge gap. It's a calibration gap.** Five papers this week, across five different research groups, methods, and domains, all converge on the same truth: people don't know how they're using AI, can't judge what's theirs vs. the AI's, experience relational dynamics they don't track, design pipelines without behavior-centered thinking, and face barriers that are about training — not technology. Practice isn't optional. It's the entire bridge. And the first exercise isn't "try this prompt" — it's "look at how you're already using AI, and calibrate."
