# Beyond Prompting Digest — 2026-07-03

**Theme: Naming What We're Actually Doing — When Words Like "Team," "Oversight," and "Understanding" Mean Different Things**

---

## Executive Summary

Four papers landed today, and they all tackle the same problem from different angles: **the words we use to describe how humans and AI work together are sloppy.** "Human-AI team" means five different things, depending on who's writing. "Oversight" usually means adversarial debate, but it works better as collaborative truth-seeking. "Understanding" can be genuine — or it can be "pseudo-rational cognition," where you mistake the AI's polished output for your own thinking. And "trust" isn't something you can assume is shared — teachers and students in the same classroom have radically different ideas about how much AI should control. Today's theme: **before you can move beyond prompting, you need precise language for what you're actually doing.** The glossary rotation is about sharpening those words.

## Concepts Discovered

### 1. Five Types of Human-AI Teams — Not One

**What it is:** Researchers analyzed 53 papers on human-AI teams and found they weren't all studying the same thing. Using psychological taxonomies of how human teams work, they identified five distinct clusters:

| Team Type | What It Looks Like | You're the... |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| **AI Assistant** | The AI supports one person. You ask, it responds. | Operator |
| **Ad-hoc Dependency** | The AI handles a step in your workflow when needed, but you're still driving. | Operator → Orchestrator |
| **Ad-hoc Forced Dependency** | The AI is the bottleneck — you HAVE to work through it. Feels constrained. | Frustrated Operator |
| **Paired Equanimity** | You and the AI work as equal partners on a shared task. Mutual back-and-forth. | Orchestrator |
| **Group Equanimity** | Multiple humans and multiple AIs work together as one team. | Architect |

**What this means in plain language:** When someone says "we have AI on our team," they could mean anything from "I use ChatGPT sometimes" to "we've designed a multi-agent system with defined roles and handoffs." The beyond-prompting shift is specifically about moving from AI Assistant and Ad-hoc Dependency toward Paired Equanimity and Group Equanimity. But you can't make that move if you don't know which team type you're in.

**Why it matters for the shift:** This taxonomy gives us language for something we've been circling for weeks. The operator is in an AI Assistant relationship. The orchestrator is in Paired Equanimity — mutual back-and-forth, shared task ownership. The architect is designing for Group Equanimity. The shift isn't just a mindset change — it's a team structure change.

**Source:** Hughes & Habli, "What Types of Human-AI Teams Exist?" (arXiv:2607.02198). 53 papers analyzed, 5 clusters identified from psychological teaming taxonomies.

### 2. Oversight Isn't Debate — It's Collaborative Truth-Seeking

**What it is:** Most AI oversight setups are adversarial: one AI argues position A, another argues position B, and a human judge picks the winner. The problem? AIs get good at being persuasive, not honest. This paper proposes "disagreement resolution" instead — drawing on human mediation and conflict resolution, where the goal isn't winning but collaboratively finding the truth. AIs work together to identify exactly where they disagree, examine the evidence together, and either converge on the right answer or isolate the specific "crux" where they genuinely differ. The result: 62.1% judging accuracy vs. 49.2% for standard debate. Nearly a 13-point improvement just by changing the interaction model from adversarial to collaborative.

**What this means in plain language:** When you're overseeing an AI agent, the instinct is to be the judge — review its output and decide if it's right. But that adversarial stance creates a dynamic where the AI (or the system) optimizes for looking correct rather than being correct. Collaborative oversight means you're not the judge — you're the mediator. You're helping the system surface its own uncertainties and work through them honestly. The difference between "prove to me this is right" and "let's figure out together what the right answer is."

**Why it matters for the shift:** The operator mode of oversight is "did the AI give me what I wanted?" The orchestrator mode is "let's work through this together to find what's actually correct." The disagreement resolution finding suggests that the orchestrator's approach — collaborative rather than adversarial — produces better results. It's evidence that the beyond-prompting mindset isn't just more pleasant — it's more accurate.

**Source:** Jiang, Chen, Wu, Sun, Liu, Feng & Tan, "Collaborative Disagreement Resolution for Scalable Oversight" (arXiv:2607.01251).

### 3. Pseudo-Rational Cognition — When You Mistake the AI's Thinking for Your Own

**What it is:** This paper introduces a critical concept: **pseudo-rational cognition** — the experience of reading an AI-generated answer, plan, or analysis and mistaking its structured, polished expression for your own rational understanding. The AI produces something that *looks* like good reasoning, and your brain registers it as *your* good reasoning. You walk away thinking "I understand this now" when what actually happened is the AI did the thinking and you did the reading. The paper also introduces **collective empiricism** — the way LLMs compress and reorganize vast amounts of human experience into outputs that feel empirical and well-evidenced, even when the underlying reasoning is shallow.

**What this means in plain language:** Ever read a ChatGPT explanation and felt smart? That feeling can be deceptive. The AI made the idea coherent — you consumed the coherence. But consuming coherence isn't the same as understanding. Pseudo-rational cognition is the trap where polished output masquerades as genuine comprehension. It's especially dangerous for people moving beyond prompting, because you're delegating more — which means you're consuming more AI-generated reasoning. Without deliberate practice, you can lose the ability to tell the difference between "I understand this" and "the AI made this look understandable."

**Why it matters for the shift:** The operator asks "did the AI give me a good answer?" The orchestrator asks "do I actually understand this, or did the AI just make it look like I do?" The paper's auditing framework — evidence-source checking, reverse questioning, practical validation — is essentially a practice regimen for the orchestrator. It's not about rejecting AI productivity. It's about making sure productivity doesn't come at the cost of genuine understanding.

**Source:** Zhao, Li & Zhang, "A Practice Auditing Framework for Large Language Model Use: Collective Empiricism, Pseudo-Rational Cognition, and Governance of AI-Generated Content" (arXiv:2607.01248).

### 4. The Trust Gap — Teachers and Students Don't See AI the Same Way

**What it is:** Researchers ran a speed-dating study with 16 students and 15 teachers in German K-12 classrooms, using storyboards to explore how each group viewed AI decision-making control. The finding: teachers and students had **misaligned views on nearly everything that matters** — how much to trust AI, how AI affects social and emotional learning, and where control should sit. What's more, the teacher-student relationship *outside* of AI use shaped how each group viewed AI inside the classroom. Trust in AI wasn't just about the technology — it was about trust in each other.

**What this means in plain language:** When you introduce AI into any group setting — a classroom, a team, a department — you're not just introducing a tool. You're introducing a third party into existing relationships. And existing relationships shape how everyone views that third party. The trust gap finding means you can't just "add AI" and assume everyone's on the same page. The teacher thinks AI should be tightly controlled; the student thinks it should be freely available. Neither is wrong — but if nobody names the gap, it becomes a source of friction, not a source of insight.

**Why it matters for the shift:** This is a glossary-rotation insight because it sharpens the word **trust.** Trust in AI isn't one thing — it's a relationship between people that gets projected onto the technology. Before you calibrate trust in an AI agent, you need to calibrate trust in the people who are using it alongside you. The orchestrator doesn't just manage agent workflows — they manage the human relationships that shape how agents are perceived.

**Source:** Nagashima, Siegrist, Scholz, Sato, Vincoli & Su, "Mind the Trust Gap: Identifying (Mis)alignments in Teacher-Student Views Toward Control and Agency in K-12 Classroom AI" (arXiv:2607.01506).

## For the Operator

You're in Phase 2 — writing prompts, iterating, staying close to the output. Today's research is about noticing something you might already feel but haven't named.

**Check which team type you're in.** The five types above aren't abstract research categories — they describe your actual relationship with AI right now. Are you in AI Assistant mode (you ask, it responds, you're in full control)? Ad-hoc Dependency (the AI handles a step here and there)? Somewhere frustrated in Ad-hoc Forced Dependency (the tool is mandatory but feels constraining)? Just naming your current team type is a step toward moving beyond it. You can't shift to Paired Equanimity if you don't know you're in AI Assistant mode.

**Watch for pseudo-rational cognition.** After your next AI interaction, ask yourself: "Do I actually understand this, or does the AI's explanation just feel coherent?" Try the reverse question test from the auditing paper: if someone asked you to explain the concept backward — starting from the conclusion and working to the premise — could you do it? If not, you might be experiencing pseudo-rational cognition. The fix isn't to stop using AI. It's to add a 60-second "explain it back to yourself in your own words" step after every significant output. That tiny practice keeps your understanding honest.

## For the Orchestrator

You're already delegating. Today's research is about refining *how* you oversee what you've delegated, and making sure your team shares language.

**Shift from adversarial to collaborative oversight.** When you review an agent's output, notice your stance. Are you judging ("is this right or wrong?") or collaborating ("what can we figure out together about whether this is right?")? The disagreement resolution paper found a 13-point accuracy gain from collaborative truth-seeking. Try this: instead of reviewing output alone and deciding, ask the agent to surface its uncertainties first. "Before I review, tell me: what parts of this are you least confident about, and why?" That question shifts the dynamic from adversarial to collaborative instantly.

**Name your team type explicitly with colleagues.** If you're orchestrating workflows that involve other humans and AI agents, use the five team types to have an explicit conversation. "Right now, I think we're operating in Ad-hoc Dependency — the AI handles individual steps when we remember to use it. I'd like to move toward Paired Equanimity on the reporting workflow — where the agent and I work as equal partners on the weekly summary." Naming the current state and the target state makes the shift discussable, debatable, and trackable. It moves "let's use AI more" from a vague intention to a concrete structural change.

**Audit for pseudo-rational cognition in your pipelines.** If your workflows produce polished, well-structured outputs — reports, analyses, recommendations — the pseudo-rational cognition trap is waiting for you. Add a step: after every significant pipeline output, write one sentence in your own words explaining the core claim and whether you genuinely understand the reasoning behind it. If you can't write that sentence, the pipeline needs an explainability upgrade — not a better prompt, but a structural change that forces the reasoning to be surfaced and checked.
