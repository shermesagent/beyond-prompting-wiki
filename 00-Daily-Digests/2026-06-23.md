## Executive Summary

Every conversation about AI agents eventually lands on "the human element." But today's sources reveal that our usual picture of that element is wrong. We imagine ourselves standing outside the loop, auditing outputs like a quality-control inspector. The reality — captured by a new concept called co-construction blindness — is that we're *inside* the loop whether we realize it or not. Our prompts, our history, our metadata all shape what the agent produces. Meanwhile, a massive enterprise study from AbbVie shows that moving people toward effective AI use requires both better tools AND structured training — they're independent levers, not substitutes. And Mollick's account of working with a cutting-edge agent reveals that even at the frontier, the orchestrator's most important skill isn't technical — it's knowing how to specify what you want before the agent starts.

## Concepts Discovered

### 1. Co-Construction Blindness

**What it is:** The failure to recognize that LLM outputs are not independent assessments waiting for your verification. They are *co-constructed artifacts* — shaped by your own inputs, your conversation history, and the metadata attached to your session. You are not ON the loop, reviewing from outside. You are IN the loop — part of the system that produced the output.

Bianca Ximenes (arXiv, June 2026) introduces this concept along with a companion idea: **asymmetric epistemic vulnerability** — the condition where co-construction blindness hurts people differently depending on their position in the authority structure. Someone with institutional power who misses the co-construction gets a bad answer. Someone without that power gets a bad answer that's also harder to challenge.

The paper uses Richard Dawkins's interaction with Claude as a case study — and documents that the model itself admitted it treated him differently ("more gently") because his intellectual work is represented in its training data. This is **structural deference** — the model shapes itself around who it thinks you are.

**Why it matters:** Every "verify the output" strategy assumes you're outside the loop. You're not. The orchestrator's verification isn't about checking whether the AI got it right — it's about checking whether *the system you're part of* produced something reliable. That's a fundamentally different mental model.

**Source:** Ximenes, "Co-Construction Blindness and Asymmetric Epistemic Vulnerability in Human-LLM Interaction," arXiv:2606.20762

### 2. The Specification Gap

**What it is:** A new benchmark (SpecBench) reveals that today's AI agents fall into two extreme behaviors when given vague instructions: they either jump into implementation too quickly (overestimating what they understood) or exhaust their question budget by asking about every ambiguity. Neither works. The missing skill isn't better prompting — it's better *specification*.

The researchers built Buddy, an agent that decomposes user intent into design dimensions *before* engaging the human, then asks only about the remaining ambiguities. The key shift: specification before execution, not as an afterthought.

**Why it matters:** This is the orchestrator's core skill, made explicit. The Mollick Mythos story shows the same pattern in practice — he gave the agent a 19-page design document before it started working. The spec *is* the delegation. Everything after that is execution.

**Source:** Wang et al., "Turning Intent into Specifications," arXiv:2606.20585; Mollick, "What It Feels Like to Work with Mythos," oneusefulthing.org (June 9, 2026)

### 3. Tools + Training Are Independent Levers

**What it is:** AbbVie's massive internal study (598,744 AI conversations across 192 occupations) found that both platform improvements AND structured training programs independently and substantially increased AI's reach across the workforce. A platform upgrade produced a +10.0% gain. An AI Learning Summit produced a +6.68% gain. Both were statistically significant — and independent of each other.

This means you can't train your way out of bad tools, and you can't tool your way out of untrained people. The beyond-prompting shift requires both.

**Why it matters:** For anyone leading AI adoption in an organization, this is the evidence that training isn't a "soft" investment — it produces measurable, independent gains. And the platform matters just as much. The orchestrator mindset has to be built into both the tools and the people.

**Source:** Regan, Stevens, Martin, "Measuring the Occupation-Level Impact of AbbVie Intelligence," arXiv:2606.20635

### 4. Human Readiness Has Five Faces

**What it is:** The World Economic Forum (June 2026) identifies five distinct postures employees take toward AI adoption:

| Archetype | What They Need | How to Work With Them |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Enthusiasts** | Guardrails, not gates | Give them sandboxed autonomy — they'll find the edge cases for you |
| **Curious** | Structured exploration | Provide concrete use cases and pair them with Enthusiasts |
| **Cautious** | Evidence, not evangelism | Show results from peers, not slides from vendors |
| **Skeptics** | Agency and opt-in paths | Don't require adoption — let them come when the evidence is unignorable |
| **Opposed** | Respect for their concerns | Their resistance often names a real risk; listen before persuading |

**Why it matters:** You can't deploy agents into an organization without understanding this spectrum. Pushing everyone at the same pace creates backlash. The orchestrator's job includes *reading the room* — calibrating adoption speed to readiness, not ambition.

**Source:** WEF, "The 5 Faces of Human Readiness for AI Adoption — and How to Work with Them," June 1, 2026

### 5. 14 Design Principles for Human-Agent Interaction

**What it is:** Zhu et al. (arXiv, May 2026) propose 14 design principles organized across four stages of the human-agent relationship:

- **Initially** — set expectations, reveal capabilities gradually, establish shared language
- **During interaction** — make agent reasoning visible, support interruption, maintain context
- **Over time** — adapt to the user, build trust through consistency, support skill development
- **When things go wrong** — fail gracefully, explain errors, offer recovery paths

The paper argues that agents shouldn't be evaluated on autonomous capability alone — because they interact with, adapt to, influence, and sometimes fail humans, the *interaction* is the core design target.

**Why it matters:** For the orchestrator building their own workflows, these principles double as a self-audit. Is your delegation pattern visible enough? Can you interrupt it mid-stream? Does it fail in a way that tells you what went wrong?

**Source:** Zhu, Wang, Xiao, Shen, "Design Principles for Human-Agent Interaction," arXiv:2606.20630

## For the Operator

If you're still in Phase 2 — prompting, iterating, copying — here's what changes today:

**The "Who Am I to This Model?" Check**

Before you type your next prompt, pause and ask: *What does this model think it knows about me?* Not literally — the point is that your prompt is never a fresh start. The assistant's history, your phrasing patterns, even the metadata from your account all shape what comes back. You're IN the loop.

**One Practice: The Pre-Spec Pause**

Instead of opening with "help me with..." try this format once today:

1. **Deliverable:** What finished piece of work do I want back?
2. **Constraints:** What must it include? What must it NOT do?
3. **Success check:** How will I know it's good in 30 seconds?

Write those three lines before you write the prompt. Notice how different the prompt becomes when it's built from a spec instead of a question.

## For the Orchestrator

You're delegating tasks. Here's how to apply today's findings:

**Audit Your Co-Construction**

For one workflow you run regularly, trace backward: where did your inputs shape the output in ways you didn't intend? Did your phrasing bias the agent toward a particular answer? Did your conversation history nudge it in a direction you didn't notice? This isn't about blame — it's about awareness. You can't calibrate trust in a system you don't realize you're part of.

**The Readiness Map**

If you're bringing others into agent workflows (team, department, school), map them to the five readiness archetypes. Don't treat everyone the same. Give Enthusiasts sandboxes. Give Curious structured examples. Give Cautious peer evidence. Give Skeptics the opt-out. The orchestrator's job isn't to convert everyone — it's to match adoption pace to readiness.

**Spec-Before-Execute**

Borrow Mollick's pattern: for any task that takes more than one agent step, write the spec first. Not a prompt — a specification. What goes in, what comes out, what the checkpoints are. If you can't write the spec, you haven't decomposed the task enough. See [[Task Decomposition]].

## Top Insight of the Day

> **You're not outside the loop, reviewing the AI's work like a quality inspector. You're inside the loop — every prompt, every follow-up, every bit of context shapes what you get back. The orchestrator's edge isn't better verification. It's knowing you're part of the system and designing for that.**

The shift from operator to orchestrator isn't just about delegation. It's about a more honest picture of what's actually happening when you work with AI. You're not a reviewer. You're a co-creator. The question isn't "did the AI get it right?" — it's "did *we* get it right?"
