## Concepts Discovered

### 1. Prompt as Safety Blanket — The Barrier Nobody Names

**What it is:** The habit of holding onto the familiar Q&A loop even when delegation would work better — not because the task requires it, but because the prompt interface *feels* safer. Three hidden costs: it caps your leverage (every separate prompt is your attention spent), it prevents you from learning delegation thinking (you can't practice if you never try), and it creates a false sense of control (real control is in designing constraints and verifying outcomes, not micromanaging conversations).

**Why it matters:** This is the sibling barrier to [[Fear of Losing Control]], but subtler. Fear is about the unknown — "what will the agent do?" Safety blanket is about the familiar — "I know how to prompt, so I'll just keep doing that." Naming both gives people two different doors into the same predicament.

**Source:** Synthesis from existing wiki concepts — Fear of Losing Control, The Just Ask ChatGPT Trap, and delegation thinking research.

### 2. Build a Tiny Pipeline — The Orchestration Leap in 15 Minutes

**What it is:** A concrete exercise that chains two delegation templates together with a defined handoff. Draws directly from real agentic workflow patterns (prompt chaining, routing, evaluator-optimizer loops from Anthropic and MIT Sloan research) but distills them to the simplest possible version: two steps, one handoff, no fancy tooling. The pipeline template includes a three-pattern handoff system: same-chat, template copy-paste, and file-based.

**Why it matters:** Delegating one task is the operator-to-orchestrator door. Chaining two tasks together is the moment you step through it. This exercise is the cheapest way to feel what orchestration actually means — the output of step one becomes the input for step two, automatically, without you in the middle.

**Source:** Agentic workflow patterns from the AI Agency Knowledgebase (Anthropic building effective agents, MIT Sloan agentic AI explainer, prompt chaining/orchestrator-worker patterns).

### 3. The Daily Standup — The Feedback Loop Every Orchestrator Needs

**What it is:** A 5-minute daily habit — three questions: what ran, what worked, what didn't — modeled on software team standups but applied to your personal delegation pipelines. Not a streak to maintain. A tool. The payoff is in the thread: after two weeks you'll know exactly which templates need tightening, which pipelines are reliable enough to reduce review frequency, and which tasks you should stop delegating.

**Why it matters:** Trying delegation and practicing it are different things. Trying is one-and-done. Practicing is iterative. The standup is the iteration engine. It's also the difference between someone who builds templates and someone whose templates actually improve over time.

**Source:** Synthesis from Agile/software development practice (daily standup pattern) applied to personal AI workflow management.

## For the Orchestrator

You're already building pipelines. Here's what today's work adds to your toolkit:

**The Daily Standup is your new maintenance habit.** You have templates and pipelines. They drift. Models change. Your needs shift. The standup catches drift on day two instead of week two. Three questions: what ran, what worked, what didn't. One fix per day. Don't fix everything — pick the single most impactful adjustment.

**The pipeline handoff patterns are worth naming explicitly.** Most pipeline breaks happen at the handoff between steps. Today's Build a Tiny Pipeline exercise gives you three patterns: same-chat (simplest, least reusable), template copy-paste (works for everything, takes 10 seconds of your time), and file-based (most automated, needs tool-capable AI). Start with template copy-paste. Automate later.

**Check your safety blanket status.** Even orchestrators can backslide — catching yourself prompting step-by-step for a task you've already templated. The [[Prompt as Safety Blanket]] page has a diagnostic: pick your most routine, most boring, most predictable AI task. Are you still prompting it step by step? If yes, the blanket is still there. Time to delegate it once and prove the world doesn't end.
