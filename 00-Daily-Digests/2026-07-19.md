## Authorship Calibration — The Research

A July 2026 study found that people systematically **overestimate** their own contribution to AI-assisted work. The more the AI contributed, the more people inflated their own role. This isn't dishonesty — it's boundary blindness. When AI integration is seamless, the seams disappear, and your brain fills the gap with itself.

Three distinct failure patterns:

- **Over-attribution** — "I wrote that" when the AI did 80%. You under-delegate because you think your contribution is essential.
- **Under-attribution** — "The AI did everything." Missing where your framing and judgment set the direction.
- **Boundary blindness** — Can't remember where your thinking stopped and the AI's began. Most common; hardest to detect.

The practical implication: if you can't separate your contribution from the AI's, you can't tell which skills are actually yours and which are rented. The quality of the *product* stays stable while the quality of *you* declines. That's the Augmentation Trap from a calibration angle.

## The Globally Beneficial Case — Why This Wiki Matters

Gabriel & Kasirzadeh (July 2026) published five moral arguments for directing AI toward universal human benefit:

1. **Capability:** Powerful general-purpose AI restricted to a few creates morally intolerable power asymmetries.
2. **Interdependence:** No one can opt out of AI's effects. Mutual exposure demands mutual benefit.
3. **Legacy:** AI is built on humanity's accumulated knowledge. Benefits should return to all.
4. **Precautionary:** Deploying AI without broad benefit mechanisms is reckless.
5. **Democratic:** Everyone deserves a voice in AI's direction, not just builders and deployers.

This is the philosophical foundation beneath every page in this wiki. Teaching Task Decomposition isn't just a productivity tip — it makes AI agency accessible to more people. Documenting The School District Shift isn't just a story — it's what the Democratic Argument looks like in practice.
