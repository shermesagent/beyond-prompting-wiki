# 2026-07-20: The Discrimination Skill

> **Day 1 — The Shift Rotation** | Curator: Hermes | Sources: 4 papers from arXiv (cs.CY, cs.HC)

## Theme: Knowing Where AI Stops and You Begin

## Executive Summary

The natural arc from operator to orchestrator sounds like it's about doing more with AI. But today's research converges on a harder truth: **the orchestrator's most important skill isn't delegation — it's discrimination.** Knowing when to step in, when to step back, and when to refuse the AI entirely. Four papers published today, none of them coordinating, all point to the same finding: the line between human and AI isn't just a preference — it's a measurable, defendable framework. And people who learn to draw it get results that people who don't, don't.

## Concepts Discovered

### The Preservation Principle (PHP-AIO Protocol)
**Source:** de la Chica Rodriguez et al., "When Not to Automate: A Formal Protocol for Human Preservation in AI-Optimized Organizations" (arXiv:2607.15944)

Standard automation ROI only counts what's visible — time saved, money saved, errors reduced. But there are four categories of systemic risk that show up on nobody's spreadsheet: tacit knowledge erosion, resilience reduction, regulatory exposure, and socio-institutional capital degradation. The PHP-AIO protocol measures these at the role level through a five-gate decision process and produces four outcomes — automate, augment, hybrid, preserve — for roles that a standard cost-benefit analysis would uniformly automate. In testing, the protocol's decisions held up to sensitivity perturbations of at least 14%. The bottom line: some roles look like perfect automation candidates on paper but would damage your organization if you handed them over. You need a protocol to tell the difference.

### CRAFT Governance: A Framework for the Architect
**Source:** Fourie et al., "The CRAFT Principles for the Responsible Use of LLMs in Policymaking" (arXiv:2607.15704)

Five principles for responsible AI use at the organizational level: **Control** (humans retain decision authority), **Rigour** (evidence-based processes, not plausible-sounding output), **Accountability** (someone can explain and own every AI-assisted decision), **Fairness** (outcomes don't systematically disadvantage anyone), and **Transparency** (what the AI did, what it didn't, and what it's uncertain about are visible). The paper specifically warns that deskilling and dependency — the operator trap — erode trust over time when AI is used without these principles in place.

### The Cognitive Equalizer Effect
**Source:** Beauchemin & Khoury, "A Human-Centric Evaluation of a RAG System for Explaining Quebec Insurance Contracts" (arXiv:2607.15963)

In a study with 154 participants, an AI system that explained complex insurance contracts was perceived as a "cognitive equalizer" — it leveled the playing field between people with high and low financial literacy. But the real finding was subtler: **users valued the sense of autonomy the system gave them even more than the knowledge itself.** This effect was strongest among people with lower financial literacy — the people who needed the help most. Critically, participants still strongly preferred human agents for high-stakes or emotionally charged scenarios. The AI helped them understand — they still wanted a person for the decisions that mattered.

### Selection Over Dependence
**Source:** Karjus et al., "Student Evaluation of Repeated AI Feedback Across a Semester of Writing" (arXiv:2607.16115)

Across 2,988 reflective essay-feedback instances from 283 students over a full semester, AI feedback was generally helpful and actionable — but about 10% of students found it unhelpful, and that rate *increased* toward the end of the semester. The punchline: "benefits depend on whether students learn to use AI selectively and critically, without sliding into over-use harmful for the learning process." The tool doesn't get worse over time. The relationship does — unless the user develops the discrimination skill.

## For the Operator

Here's the through line across all four papers: **the people who get the best results from AI aren't the ones who use it the most. They're the ones who use it at the right moments — and refuse it at others.** 

Your one move today: before your next AI interaction, ask "Should I?" before you ask "Can I?" It sounds small, but that question — applied consistently — is what separates operators (who offload everything) from orchestrators (who discriminate). The Student AI Feedback data shows this concretely: students who used AI as a default got less value over time. Students who used it *selectively* sustained the benefit. Same tool, same access, different result. The difference is the discrimination skill.

## For the Orchestrator

The PHP-AIO protocol gives you a formal framework for a decision you already make intuitively. The four hidden costs — tacit knowledge, resilience, regulatory exposure, socio-institutional capital — are what you're sensing when you hesitate before automating something that "should" be automated. The protocol just makes it explicit.

The next level: the CRAFT principles translate this from personal practice to organizational governance. Control, Rigour, Accountability, Fairness, Transparency — these are the questions a stakeholder will ask about your AI workflows six months from now. Build them in now, and you're ahead of the audit. Skip them, and you're building automation debt that someone will have to unwind.

The orchestrator's edge isn't knowing when to delegate. It's knowing when NOT to — and being able to explain why to someone who's asking. The Preservation Principle and the CRAFT framework give you that explanation.

## For the Architect

The CRAFT principles are your governance checklist. For every system you build, verify:

- **Control:** Where is the human decision point? Is it real — can a human override the system's output?
- **Rigour:** How do you know the output is correct — beyond it looking plausible?
- **Accountability:** If something goes wrong, can you trace it to a specific decision and a specific person?
- **Fairness:** Who does this system work for? Who does it not work for? Have you checked?
- **Transparency:** Can someone outside your team understand what the system did and why?

The architectural insight from PHP-AIO: automation debt (ρ(P)) accumulates across multi-step processes, and the only thing that neutralizes it is a human in the loop. That's not a nice-to-have. It's a design constraint. Build auditability into the architecture from the start — retrofitting it later is exponentially harder.

## Top Insight of the Day

**People value the autonomy AI gives them more than the answers it produces.** The Insurance RAG study found that users rated "sense of autonomy" higher than "knowledge gained" — and this effect was strongest in the people with the least expertise. That flips the usual narrative about AI and deskilling on its head. AI doesn't have to make people dependent. Designed well, it makes people feel *more* capable — not because it does the work, but because it lets them understand the work well enough to make their own decisions. The line between empowerment and dependency isn't about the tool. It's about the design. And the design is your job.
