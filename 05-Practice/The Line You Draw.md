# The Line You Draw

## What You'll Do

Decide — on paper, explicitly — what you *won't* delegate to AI, and why. Not as a permanent rule, but as a starting point you'll revisit. Think of it as drawing a line in pencil, not pen.

This takes about 5 minutes. You'll walk away with clarity about where your delegation practice should focus — and where it shouldn't.

---

## Why This Matters

Most people approach delegation as a question of *can I?* — can the AI do this task? Can it do it well enough? That's the operator's question. The orchestrator's question is different: *should I?*

Research on 448 professionals at Microsoft found that delegation boundaries aren't random. People draw lines around three kinds of work:

- **Identity work:** Tasks that feel like "who I am as a professional." Writing, designing, strategizing, mentoring. Handing these off feels like handing off part of yourself.
- **Human-facing work:** Tasks where someone else will see the output and judge *you* for it. Client presentations, stakeholder updates, performance reviews.
- **Design-oriented work:** Tasks that require taste, not just correctness. Brand voice, visual choices, creative direction.

The researchers also found that two things make people *more* willing to delegate over time: AI experience and risk tolerance. Both grow with practice. The line you draw today is real — but it's also movable. The practice is knowing where it is and checking on it.

## The Line-Drawing Exercise

Take 5 minutes. Be honest. No one else will see this.

```
THE LINE I DRAW — [Today's Date]

THREE THINGS I WON'T DELEGATE TO AI:

1. _____________________________________________________
   Why? (circle the strongest reason)
   Identity | Accountability | Taste | Fear | Other: ____

2. _____________________________________________________
   Why? (circle the strongest reason)
   Identity | Accountability | Taste | Fear | Other: ____

3. _____________________________________________________
   Why? (circle the strongest reason)
   Identity | Accountability | Taste | Fear | Other: ____

ONE THING I MIGHT BE WRONG ABOUT:
_____________________________________________________
(Which of the three above could you imagine changing your mind about
after 3 months of practice? Write it here. If the answer is "none,"
that's data too — but sit with it for a moment.)

TWO-MONTH CHECK-IN DATE:
I'll revisit this list on: _______________
```

---

## Step by Step

### Step 1 — Brainstorm (2 min)

Think about your actual work. Not abstract "what AI could do" — the tasks you did yesterday, the ones you'll do tomorrow. Which of those would you feel uncomfortable handing to an AI, even if the AI could do them perfectly?

Don't filter. Write down everything that comes to mind. Then pick the three that feel strongest.

### Step 2 — Name the Why (1 min)

For each item, circle the strongest reason. The Microsoft study found that "identity" and "accountability" are the most common — but your reasons might be different. Fear is real and worth naming. "Other" is fine, but try to put words to it in the margin.

The point isn't to judge your reasons. It's to *see* them. You can't practice crossing a boundary you can't name.

### Step 3 — Find the Movable One (1 min)

Look at your three lines. Is there one that feels less permanent than the others? One where you can imagine — with practice, with experience, with the right safety net — eventually delegating? Write it down.

If the answer is honestly "none of them," that's fine. But note it explicitly: "None feel movable right now." That's your starting point. Come back in two months and see if anything shifted.

### Step 4 — Set a Check-In Date (1 min)

Pick a date roughly two months from now. Put it in your calendar. "Revisit delegation boundaries." When that date arrives, pull out this sheet and ask:

- Have I actually delegated any of these since I wrote them down?
- Has my experience with AI changed since then?
- Do any of these lines feel different now?

## Example

Here's what a completed exercise might look like for a marketing director mid-shift:

```
THE LINE I DRAW — July 2, 2026

THREE THINGS I WON'T DELEGATE TO AI:

1. Brand voice guidelines and tone decisions
   Why: Identity — "This is my craft. I built this voice."

2. Client-facing presentations and pitches
   Why: Accountability — "If the pitch fails, it's on me, not the AI."

3. Strategic direction for quarterly planning
   Why: Identity — "This is literally my job description."

ONE THING I MIGHT BE WRONG ABOUT:
Client-facing presentations. I've seen AI draft solid decks.
The real fear is that the AI will miss a nuance — but I could
review and catch it. Maybe the line here is "AI drafts, I deliver,"
not "I do everything from scratch."

TWO-MONTH CHECK-IN DATE: September 2, 2026
```

## The Quarterly Boundary Audit

Once you've done the initial exercise, the practice becomes periodic. Every three months, pull out your last audit and ask:

1. **What moved?** Which boundary shifted — either you started delegating something you said you wouldn't, or you realized a delegated task should come back to you?

2. **What stayed?** Which boundary held firm, and is that because of a real, examined reason or because you haven't revisited it?

3. **What surprised you?** Did any delegation feel easier than you expected? Harder? Did you discover a boundary you didn't know you had?

4. **What's the next edge?** What's one thing that currently feels "too identity-close" or "too accountability-heavy" that you want to practice toward — not delegate tomorrow, but start experimenting with?

The quarterly audit isn't a test. It's a compass check. You're not trying to eliminate your boundaries. You're trying to make them *intentional*.

## How This Fits the Practice Progression

- **Week 1 (Audit Your Prompts):** Do this exercise alongside your prompt audit. The prompt audit tells you what you *could* delegate. The line exercise tells you what you *shouldn't* — yet. Together, they give you a map with both destinations and boundaries marked.
- **Week 2 (First Delegation):** Your line exercise tells you where *not* to start. Pick a delegation candidate that's clearly on the safe side of your line. Build confidence there first.
- **Week 3 (Build a Tiny Pipeline):** As pipelines grow, some will cross your lines. That's expected. Revisit this exercise when a pipeline pushes against a boundary you set.
- **Daily Standup:** Add one question to your standup: "Did I delegate across any of my lines today — intentionally or accidentally?" The noticing is the practice.

## Common Pitfalls

**"All of my boundaries are identity-based. I feel like I *am* my work."**
That's not a problem — that's a starting point. The Microsoft study found identity-based boundaries are the most common and the strongest. Start with the smallest, least identity-close task you can find. Practice there. Let the confidence spread outward.

**"I couldn't think of anything I wouldn't delegate."**
Two possibilities: either you're unusually comfortable with delegation (possible, especially if you have high AI experience), or you haven't thought hard enough about what matters to you (more common). Try the exercise anyway — write down three things you'd delegate *last*, even if you'd delegate everything eventually. The order is the line.

**"My boundaries changed the first week. Was the exercise useless?"**
The opposite. The exercise worked. You made boundaries explicit, practiced near them, and they moved. That's the whole point. Draw new ones and keep going.

**"I don't want to delegate — I like doing the work myself."**
That's valid. The beyond-prompting shift isn't about delegating everything. It's about having the *option*, and choosing intentionally. If you choose to keep certain tasks because you genuinely enjoy them, that's not a boundary to overcome — it's a preference to honor.
