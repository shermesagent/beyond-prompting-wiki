## Summary

Loss of control risks have gained prominence in public discussion about AI, but the concept rests on weak foundations — even those who discuss it extensively don't first establish what control is and what exactly is being lost. This paper addresses those gaps.

## Key Findings

- **Definition of control:** anchored to the "setting and getting of goals"
- Control requires: the ability to set goals, a functional control loop, requisite variety, and sufficient goal alignment
- The paper discusses how control can be lost, how AIs can contribute to such loss, and offers recommendations for maintaining control
- Draws on foundational concepts from cybernetics, management control, and control theory

## Why It Matters

For the operator→orchestrator shift, this paper provides the framework for what people actually fear when they say "I'll lose control": it's not that the AI will go rogue — it's that the goal-setting loop will break. The orchestrator's job is to maintain a functional control loop: set goals, delegate execution, verify results, adjust. The paper gives a vocabulary for what that loop needs to stay intact. One interesting consequence: humans can maintain control even when delegating extensively, as long as the goal-setting and verification layers stay human.
