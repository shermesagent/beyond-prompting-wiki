# When AI Blurs the Boundaries of Contribution: An Empirical Study of Authorship Calibration

**arXiv:** 2607.15006
**Category:** cs.HC

## Abstract

This empirical study investigates "authorship calibration" — the accuracy with which people judge their own contribution to AI-assisted work. The key finding: heavy AI users systematically misjudge their own level of contribution. When AI plays a significant role in producing output, users tend to overestimate their own authorship and underestimate the AI's contribution.

This creates a specific problem for the operator-to-orchestrator shift: if you don't accurately perceive how much the AI is doing, you can't make good decisions about what to delegate, what to keep, and where your own value actually lies.

The study identifies three patterns of miscalibration:
1. **Over-attribution** — Taking credit for AI-generated work as if it were primarily your own
2. **Under-attribution** — Failing to recognize where your uniquely human contribution (judgment, framing, direction-setting) made the difference
3. **Boundary blindness** — Losing track of where your thinking ended and the AI's began

## Key Implications

Authorship calibration is a learnable skill. The study suggests that deliberate reflection on *who contributed what* — especially after complex AI collaborations — can improve calibration accuracy over time. This is directly relevant to the Audit Your Prompts practice.
