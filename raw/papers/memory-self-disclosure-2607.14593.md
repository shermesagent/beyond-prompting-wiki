# Memory-Driven Self-Disclosure and Relational Turning Points: A Longitudinal Multimodal Study of Human-AI Interaction

**arXiv:** 2607.14593
**Category:** cs.HC

## Abstract

This longitudinal study tracked human-AI interactions over 10 sessions to understand how the *relationship* develops — not just the task performance. Key findings:

1. **Conversational quality predicts enjoyment** — but enjoyment in one session doesn't carry forward to the next. Each session is partially a fresh start.

2. **AI memory predicts self-disclosure** — when the AI remembers things from previous sessions, users disclose more personal information over time. Memory creates relational continuity.

3. **Turning points are real** — Users experience identifiable "crashes" (moments where the AI fails, forgets, or breaks the relational frame) and "surges" (moments where the AI surprises with insight or empathy). These turning points reshape the relationship trajectory.

4. **Ten sessions is enough for a pattern** — by the end of the study, most users had developed a stable relational pattern with the AI, for better or worse.

## Key Implications

Sustained AI delegation isn't just a technical practice — it's a relational one. How you *feel* about the AI after 10 sessions shapes what you'll delegate, how much you'll trust, and whether you'll persist when it fails. The Daily Standup practice should track not just output quality but relational quality.
