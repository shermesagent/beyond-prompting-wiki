# The twilight of the chatbots

**Author:** Ethan Mollick
**Published:** June 30, 2026
**Source:** One Useful Thing (Substack)

## Summary

AI capability is accelerating at a better-than-exponential rate across multiple independent measurements. The key shift: AI systems are no longer just chatbots that answer questions — they are autonomous workers that can execute complex tasks for hours without human intervention.

## Key Points

- **Accelerating capability curves:** METR, the UK AISI, and GDPval all show AI's ability to do real work growing faster than exponentially. The time horizon for autonomous work keeps extending.
- **Autonomous work benchmarks:**
  - GPT-5.5 can work autonomously for 2+ hours on complex tasks
  - Opus 4.8 can work for 9+ hours independently (though paused by government interventions alongside GPT-5.6)
  - Epoch found Opus 4.7, working alone for 14 hours, built a software package that would take 2-17 weeks of human engineering work — for $251 in tokens
- **The twilight of chatbots:** We are moving past the era where AI is primarily interacted with through a chat interface. The new paradigm is giving AI a task and letting it work autonomously.
- **Chinese open-weights models** (Qwen, DeepSeek, etc.) are on their own exponential curve, trailing US frontier models by 6-12 months. They are open weights — anyone can use or modify them.
- **Two-tier system emerging:** Proprietary US frontier models (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google) and open-weights near-frontier models (China), both improving exponentially.
- **Talent implications:** As AI systems can autonomously handle more hours of work, the value shifts from "can you operate AI?" to "can you design systems where AI operates?"

## Relevance to Beyond Prompting

This is the core evidence that the operator→orchestrator shift is being driven by capability, not just philosophy. When AI can work autonomously for 9+ hours on complex tasks, the job is no longer about writing better prompts — it's about designing delegation systems, setting constraints, and verifying outputs at checkpoints.
