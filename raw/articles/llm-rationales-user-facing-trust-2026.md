# When LLM Rationales Become User-Facing: Effects on Trust Perception, Decision-Making, and Gaze Behaviors

Xin Sun, Ting Pan, Yajing Wang, Shu Wei, Jos A. Bosch, Isao Echizen, Abdallah El Ali, Saku Sugawara
arXiv:2606.25489, June 24, 2026

**Key findings:**
- Study 1 (N=68 online): Tested rationale presentation format (instant, delayed, on demand), correctness (correct, incorrect), and certainty framing (none, certain, uncertain). No reliable format effects; correctness and certainty framing influenced trust in information, trust in LLM system, and decision confidence.
- Study 2 (N=54 eye-tracking): Incorrect rationales drew more attention to supporting evidence and larger pupil diameter — more cognitive effort. Incorrect rationales lowered trust in LLM system relative to no rationale. No-rationale difference was weaker for trust in information.

**Design implication:** "More reasoning is not always better." Selective, evidence-linked, certainty-calibrated rationales are superior to dumping full chain-of-thought. User-facing rationales should help people inspect whether an answer is warranted by evidence — not just persuade through fluent reasoning.
