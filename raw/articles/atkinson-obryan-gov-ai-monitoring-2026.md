# Government AI Use as a Monitoring Primitive: A Public Document Pilot Study

**Authors:** David I. Atkinson, Joan Eleanor O'Bryan
**Date:** July 7, 2026
**Source:** arXiv:2607.04543 (cs.CY)

## Abstract

Governments are important actors in frontier AI governance, but many facts about their adoption and use of AI systems are difficult to observe directly. Procurement disclosures and official statements are useful, but can also be delayed, selective, and better suited to measuring formal adoption than actual day-to-day use.

The authors propose a complementary monitoring primitive: measuring traces of language-model assistance in public government documents. The approach is lightweight, externally reproducible, and based on revealed behavior rather than stated intent.

In a pilot study of ten public document streams from U.S. and PRC government-related sources, they find that, while 2021 baselines are consistently near zero, by 2026, four of ten sources show statistically significant signs of AI-assisted writing. In their sample, the U.S. signal concentrates in publications downstream of policy work; the PRC signal concentrates closer to it.

## Key Findings

- **4 of 10 government document sources** show statistically significant AI-assisted writing by 2026 (up from near-zero baselines in 2021)
- **U.S. pattern:** AI traces appear in publications downstream of policy work (reports, summaries, communications) — not in policy-making itself
- **PRC pattern:** AI traces appear closer to policy work
- **The approach is lightweight:** Uses detectable traces of AI writing, not self-reported adoption — measures revealed behavior, not stated intent
- **Implication:** Government AI adoption is real, measurable, and growing — but uneven across functions and countries
