# Against "Brain Damage"

**Author:** Ethan Mollick
**Source:** One Useful Thing (Substack)

## Summary

AI doesn't damage our brains — but unthinking use can damage our thinking. The research across three domains (learning, creativity, collaboration) shows the same pattern: AI helps or hurts depending on HOW you use it, and the key variable is sequencing — whether you think first before turning to AI.

## Key Points

### Learning & Education
- It's how you USE AI, not whether you use it, that determines if it helps or hurts your brain
- The default AI behavior gives answers rather than tutoring — which produces worse learning outcomes
- A deliberate tutor prompt (asking AI to explain without giving answers) produces better results
- Parents can prompt: "explain this in a way I can teach my child, who is in X grade"

### Creativity
- AI beats 91% of humans on creativity tests (Alternative Uses Test), 99% on Torrance Tests
- BUT: AI acts like "a single creative person with predictable patterns" — ideas converge on VR, blockchain, environment, and AI itself
- The paradox: AI is more creative than most individuals, but lacks the diversity that comes from multiple perspectives
- Better prompting generates more diverse ideas, but doesn't fully close the gap to human group diversity
- The **anchoring effect**: once you see AI's ideas, it becomes much harder to think outside those boundaries
- People also feel less ownership in AI-generated ideas → disengagement from the ideation process

### Writing & Thinking
- For many people, "writing IS thinking" — the act of writing and rewriting develops ideas
- If you let AI handle your writing, you skip the thinking part entirely
- Mollick's personal practice: full draft without AI, then use AI as reader/editor only

### Collective Work & Meetings
- AI meeting summaries raise existential questions: "why are we meeting in the first place?"
- The alternative: AI as facilitator, not replacement (e.g., tarot-card-style facilitation)
- AI can surface unspoken concerns, identify unheard voices, find patterns in team dynamics

## The Core Principle: Sequencing

The research gives a clear answer across all three domains: **think first, write first, meet first.** Then use AI to refine, expand, and improve. The order matters.

- Generate your own ideas before asking AI for suggestions
- Do a full draft before using AI as editor
- Have the meeting before reading the AI summary

## Closing Quote

"Your brain is safe. Your thinking, however, is up to you."
