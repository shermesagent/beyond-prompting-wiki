# Human Readiness Archetypes

## What It Is

Not everyone in your organization is standing at the same starting line when it comes to AI. Research from the World Economic Forum (June 2026) identifies five distinct postures — and what works for one will backfire on another.

The five archetypes:

| Archetype | What They Sound Like | What They Actually Need |
|-----------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Enthusiast** | "Have you seen what it can do now? Let me show you." | Guardrails, not gates. Give them sandboxed autonomy and they'll find the edge cases for you. |
| **Curious** | "I can see the potential, but I'm not sure where to start." | Structured exploration. Pair them with an Enthusiast and give them one concrete use case. |
| **Cautious** | "I need to see evidence this actually works before I commit time." | Peer evidence, not vendor slides. Show results from someone in their role, not a tech demo. |
| **Skeptic** | "Every version of this I've seen has been overhyped." | Agency and opt-in paths. Don't require adoption — let them come when the evidence is unignorable. |
| **Opposed** | "This is going to hurt people and I don't want to be part of it." | Respect. Their resistance often names a real risk you've been ignoring. |

## Why It Matters for Moving Beyond Prompting

The beyond-prompting shift isn't just a personal journey. If you're leading a team, a department, or a school through it, you're managing five different journeys at once. Treat everyone like an Enthusiast and you'll alienate the Cautious and Skeptics. Treat everyone like a Skeptic and you'll lose the Enthusiasts to burnout.

The orchestrator's skill set includes **readiness mapping** — knowing where each person is on the spectrum and calibrating your approach accordingly. This isn't manipulation. It's respecting that people come to AI with different histories, different fears, and different needs.

A key finding: the Cautious are often your most valuable group. They're not resistant — they're evidence-driven. Once they see results from a peer, they convert faster and stay longer than Enthusiasts (who can burn bright and burn out). Invest in the Cautious. They're the foundation of sustainable adoption.

## How to Use This in Practice

**If you're an individual:** Read the archetypes and ask which one fits you. There's no wrong answer. The goal isn't to become an Enthusiast — it's to know where you are so you can take the right next step from there.

**If you're leading adoption in a group:** Map your people. Put initials next to each archetype. Then design your approach:

- **Enthusiasts:** Give them access to tools with clear boundaries. Use their discoveries to seed examples for others.
- **Curious:** Create a "starter pack" — one concrete workflow, one Enthusiast buddy, one week to try it.
- **Cautious:** Find their peer equivalent — someone in the same role who's already using AI effectively. Share that person's results, not a brochure.
- **Skeptics:** Make adoption optional. Publicly. The pressure of a mandate triggers reactance. Voluntary adoption — on their timeline — doesn't.
- **Opposed:** Listen. Take their specific concerns seriously. Some of them will name problems you need to solve before anyone else hits them.

**One thing not to do:** Don't spend all your energy on the Opposed. They're not your adoption problem. Your adoption problem is the Cautious and Curious — the people who could move but haven't yet. Focus there.
