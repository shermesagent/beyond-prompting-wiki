# The Placement Rule

## What It Is

**If letting AI in makes the task feel effortless, it is in the wrong place.**

That's the placement rule. It comes from new research (arXiv, June 2026) that tested the same AI model with students in three different configurations. Unguarded AI helper: students scored 17% worse. Guarded AI (same model, built to withhold answers): harm erased. Engineered tutor (same model, designed to scaffold without solving): roughly doubled learning. Same AI. Same students. Same subject. The only variable: *where in the thinking process the AI was placed.*

Learning requires productive struggle. Remove the struggle — by putting AI where the thinking is supposed to happen — and you remove the learning. Everything *feels* smoother. You get the answer faster. But you didn't get stronger. That's the illusion: confident mastery that collapses when the AI isn't there.

The placement rule is a diagnostic, not a ban. It doesn't say "don't use AI." It says "use AI *after* the thinking has happened, not *instead* of it."

## Why It Matters for Moving Beyond Prompting

The operator's instinct is to put AI everywhere — first draft, first idea, first attempt. It feels efficient. It *is* efficient, in the moment. But efficiency without placement is just slow self-replacement.

The orchestrator thinks differently. For every step in a workflow, they ask: **"Is this where thinking needs to happen, or where thinking has already happened?"** AI goes in the second category. Human judgment stays in the first.

This is what separates [[The Orchestrator Mindset]] from [[The Operator Mindset]] at the workflow level. The operator asks "can AI do this step?" The orchestrator asks "should AI do this step, and if so, *how* — as a crutch or as a coach?"

The placement rule connects directly to:
- [[The Augmentation Trap]] — AI in the wrong place *feels* productive while eroding skill
- [[Friction by Design]] — the right place often involves deliberate friction
- [[The Coaching Stance]] — the six-move model is a placement framework for learning
- [[Task Decomposition]] — decomposition IS placement: deciding which pieces go where

## The Three Zones

Every workflow has three placement zones:

| Zone | What Happens Here | AI's Role | The Test |
|------|-------------------|-----------|----------|
| **Human-Only Zone** | First attempt, direction-setting, final verification | None — you do the thinking | If AI made this feel effortless, it's in the wrong place |
| **Guarded AI Zone** | Scaffolding, iteration, exploration | AI supports but doesn't solve — asks questions back, withholds answers | If you can't explain the logic without the AI, it's doing too much |
| **Automation Zone** | Routine formatting, data transformation, scheduled tasks | AI runs freely | If this DOESN'T feel effortless, the automation isn't working yet |

An operator has everything in the automation zone — or tries to. An orchestrator deliberately maintains all three, moving tasks between zones as their own capability grows.

## The Six-Move Model

The researchers behind the placement rule also gave us a practical sequence for learning any new skill with AI. The first and last moves are always human-only:

1. **Prime** (HUMAN) — Activate what you already know. What do you think the answer might be? What's your gut instinct?
2. **Probe** (GUARDED AI) — AI asks you questions. It doesn't give answers yet. What are you missing? What assumptions are you making?
3. **Point** (GUARDED AI) — AI directs your attention to specific gaps or patterns. Not "here's the answer" but "look more closely at this part."
4. **Attach** (GUARDED AI) — AI connects new information to what you already know. "This new concept is like that concept you already understand, except for this difference."
5. **Strengthen** (GUARDED AI) — AI provides practice, variation, and spaced repetition. You're doing the work; AI is varying the challenge.
6. **Test** (HUMAN) — Unaided check. Can you do it without the AI? If not, go back to move 4.

The placement rule lives in every move: the human-only moves should feel effortful. The guarded AI moves should feel supported but not solved. If any guarded move feels effortless, the AI has crossed the line from coach to crutch.

## How to Spot It in Your Day

You're applying the placement rule when:

- You can point to specific steps in your workflow and say "AI belongs here, not there"
- You have at least one human-only step before AI touches anything
- When AI output arrives, you compare it to what you expected, not just whether it "looks good"
- Tasks that used to feel hard still feel hard — but you're faster at them than you were
- You occasionally catch yourself thinking "wait, let me try this myself first"

You're NOT applying the placement rule when:

- AI is your first move on every task, every time
- Workflows feel effortless from start to finish
- You couldn't describe your thinking process without referencing what the AI told you
- "Using AI well" means "getting answers faster" rather than "getting sharper at finding answers"

## Try This

**5-Minute Exercise: Find Your Misplaced AI**

1. List the three tasks you use AI for most often.
2. For each one, ask the placement question: **"If I did the first step without AI, would it feel harder? If yes, AI might be in the wrong place."**
3. Pick the task where the answer is most clearly "yes." This is your misplaced AI.
4. Tomorrow, for that task only: do the first step yourself. Sketch the outline, draft the opening, list the questions. *Then* bring in AI to scaffold the rest.
5. At the end, ask: was the final result better? Did YOU feel sharper? My bet: yes to both.

**The "Move AI Later" Week**

This is a one-week experiment. For every AI interaction:
- Day 1-2: Do the first step yourself. Then AI.
- Day 3-4: Do the first AND last step yourself. AI in the middle only.
- Day 5: Do a full task without AI. Compare how it feels to Day 1.

By Day 5, you'll have a map of where AI belongs in your thinking — not because someone told you, but because you felt the difference.

## The Research Behind This

- **The Effortless Trap (Brcic & Frljic, arXiv, June 2026):** Causal evidence — unguarded AI = 17% worse exam scores; guarded AI = neutral; engineered tutor = ~2x learning. The six-move model and placement rule emerge from this data. The key mechanism: productive struggle is necessary for learning, and AI that removes struggle removes learning.

- **Pingquanqi / Equalizer (Yu Wang, arXiv, June 2026):** A governance framework for human-agent interaction that includes controlled friction mechanisms, Bayesian stop-loss rules, and a token-to-lifetime transparency metric. Provides the infrastructure-level thinking behind the placement rule.

- **The Augmentation Trap (Caosun & Aral, arXiv, April 2026):** The structural model showing why people rationally put AI in the wrong place — short-term gains mask long-term erosion. The placement rule is the counter-strategy.
