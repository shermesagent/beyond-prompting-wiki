# The Four Decision Labels

## What It Is

Before you touch AI for any task, there's one question that changes everything: **which bucket does this go in?**

Not "what prompt should I use?" Not "which model is best?" Just: what kind of human-AI work is this?

The Four Decision Labels give you a simple framework:

| Label | What It Means | When to Use It |
|-------|--------------|----------------|
| **Automate** | The AI does it. You verify. | Low-risk, repetitive, easily checked tasks |
| **Augment** | AI drafts or suggests. You own the final call. | Tasks where judgment matters but the heavy lifting is mechanical |
| **Human-Only** | No AI at all. You do it yourself. | Tasks where relationship, values, or embodied context are central |
| **Prohibit AI** | AI use is not allowed — by policy, law, or ethics. | Tasks where privacy, safety, or dignity make AI inappropriate |

The power isn't in memorizing the labels. It's in the **pause** — the moment before you open a chat window where you ask "which bucket?" instead of defaulting to "let me just type something and see what happens."

Most people — skilled operators included — live entirely in the "augment" bucket. Every task gets the same treatment: type a prompt, review the output, edit, repeat. The orchestrator knows that different buckets call for different approaches.

## Why It Matters for Moving Beyond Prompting

The four labels are a delegation decision tool, not a productivity hack. Here's what they unlock:

**They force you to think before you prompt.** The operator's reflex is "open chat, type instruction." The orchestrator's reflex is "classify the task, then choose the approach." That half-second pause — automate? augment? human? prohibit? — is the seed of every delegation decision you'll make.

**They surface hidden automation opportunities.** When you audit your AI use through these labels, you'll find tasks you've been "augmenting" (prompt → review → edit → prompt → review → edit) that could be "automated" (one spec, one run, one check). Every task you move from augment to automate is leverage gained.

**They reveal where your judgment is irreplaceable.** Tasks labeled "human-only" are where you add the value that no AI can replicate — relationships, ethical trade-offs, contextual wisdom. Naming these protects you from the augmentation trap: delegating away the very skills that make you valuable.

**They catch the tasks AI shouldn't touch.** Prohibiting AI for sensitive tasks — student records, personnel decisions, mental health support — isn't a failure of the framework. It's the framework doing its job. Good delegation includes knowing what not to delegate.

## The Labels in Practice

### Automate

> *"This runs the same way every time. If it breaks, I'll know."*

**Good candidates:** Formatting reports, tagging and sorting emails, generating weekly summaries from structured data, cross-referencing information against a known database, running scheduled checks.

**The test:** Can you write a spec for this task that a reasonable person could follow? If yes, AI can probably automate it. The spec doesn't need to be perfect — it needs to be clear enough that failures are obvious.

**The risk:** Automating something that isn't actually stable. If the inputs change subtly, the output degrades silently. Keep one human check per cycle — not per step, per cycle.

### Augment

> *"AI does the draft. I make the call."*

**Good candidates:** Writing recommendations, analyzing data, creating lesson plans, drafting proposals, summarizing research, generating options for a decision.

**The test:** Does the final output need your judgment — your knowledge of context, your taste, your values? If yes, augment. The AI handles the heavy lifting. You handle the direction.

**The risk:** Augmenting everything by default. Augmentation has a cost: your attention. Every augmented task requires your review. If you're augmenting 20 tasks a day, you're spending your attention on review, not direction. Move the stable ones to automate.

### Human-Only

> *"This only works if I'm the one doing it."*

**Good candidates:** Difficult feedback conversations, ethical trade-off decisions, strategic vision-setting, creative direction where taste is the entire product, mentoring and coaching moments.

**The test:** Would removing yourself from this task make the outcome worse in ways that matter? If yes, it's human-only.

**The risk:** Labeling too many things human-only because you're uncomfortable delegating. Discomfort is not the same as irreplaceability. Test it: if you delegated this once, and the AI got it 80% right, would the result be unacceptable? If yes, it's truly human-only. If you're just uneasy, try delegating once and see.

### Prohibit AI

> *"Using AI here would be wrong, not just inefficient."*

**Good candidates:** Handling sensitive personal data without consent, making final hiring/firing decisions, providing mental health support, any task where regulatory compliance explicitly forbids AI use, situations where dignity requires a human presence.

**The test:** If someone found out AI was involved in this task, would they feel violated? If yes, prohibit it. The goal isn't to use AI everywhere it *can* go. It's to use AI where it *should* go.

**The risk:** Prohibiting AI for tasks where it could actually help — often out of fear or institutional inertia. Distinguish between "AI shouldn't do this" (prohibit) and "I'm not ready to delegate this" (keep in augment for now, revisit next quarter).

## How to Spot It in Your Day

You're using the four labels well when:

- You pause before every AI session and classify the task
- You notice tasks you've been augmenting that should be automated
- You can name at least three human-only tasks in your week — and why
- You've had at least one conversation about what AI should NOT touch
- Your default isn't "augment" anymore — it's "classify first, then act"

You're still in default mode when:

- Every AI task gets the same treatment: prompt → review → edit
- You've never explicitly labeled a task "human-only"
- You can't think of anything you'd prohibit AI from touching
- The idea of automating something makes you uncomfortable, but you can't say why

## Try This

**The 10-Minute Label Audit**

1. Take out a piece of paper (or open a note). Draw four columns: Automate, Augment, Human-Only, Prohibit.

2. Think through your last three days of work. Write every task you used AI for — or thought about using AI for — in one of the four columns. Don't overthink it. First instinct.

3. Now look at your "augment" column. Is it the longest? (It almost always is.) Pick one task from that column and ask: "Could this be automated?" Not "is it ready right now" — just "could it be?" Put a star next to any task where the answer is yes.

4. Look at your "human-only" column. Is it empty? If so, you might be over-delegating — or you might not have thought hard enough about what only you can do. Add at least one task that genuinely requires you.

5. Pick the starred task from step 3. This week, automate it. Not perfectly. Just once. See what breaks.
