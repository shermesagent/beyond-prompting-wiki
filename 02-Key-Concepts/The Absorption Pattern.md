# The Absorption Pattern

## What It Is

The Absorption Pattern is the process by which generative AI redirects developmental work — the tasks that build human expertise through productive struggle — into AI workflows, leaving the human less capable over time. The AI doesn't just do the work. It absorbs the pathway that would have built the next generation of competence.

Researchers Zhang, Zhang & Sun (arXiv, July 2026) named this pattern through 14 interviews with juniors and seniors in South Korean software engineering. They found that GenAI doesn't just eliminate specific tasks — it absorbs the developmental *trajectory* through which juniors become seniors. The work that used to build expertise (the tedious debugging, the first-draft writing, the struggle to get something working) gets redirected into senior-AI workflows.

Three consequences follow:

1. **Productive struggle is lost.** Juniors never experience the friction that forces them to develop judgment.
2. **Structural reproduction.** Universities normalize GenAI use in classrooms, so the absorption becomes institutional — not just individual.
3. **Perceptual asymmetry.** Seniors don't see the loss because they already have the expertise. Juniors don't see the loss because they never knew what they were missing. Neither side can correct a dynamic neither side can see.

## Why It Matters for Moving Beyond Prompting

The Absorption Pattern is the mechanism behind [[The Augmentation Trap]]. The trap works through absorption: AI front-loads productivity while silently absorbing the developmental tasks that would have built the user's expertise. The user feels productive. The organization sees output. But capability is being redirected — not built.

This matters for the orchestrator at three levels:

**At the individual level:** Every task you delegate is either developmental (it builds your judgment) or non-developmental (it doesn't). When you absorb a developmental task into AI, you trade this week's speed for next year's weakness. The orchestrator discriminates between the two. See [[Task Decomposition]] for the Absorption Check.

**At the team level:** The Absorption Pattern is structural — it doesn't just happen to individuals. When an organization normalizes AI use without preserving developmental pathways, it's engineering its own expertise shortage. The seniors get faster. The pipeline that would have produced the next seniors gets absorbed. Five years later, you have a capability cliff and no one who can climb it.

**At the ecosystem level:** The Informal Learning study (Honeycutt et al., 128K conversations) found that only 4.9% of LLM interactions involve constructive engagement — the deep sense-making that builds capability. The other 95%+ is answer delivery. If the dominant mode of human-AI interaction doesn't build human capability, the Absorption Pattern isn't a bug in a few workflows — it's a property of the ecosystem.

## How to Spot It in Your Day

The Absorption Pattern is hard to spot because it masquerades as productivity. Signs to watch for:

- **Tasks you used to find challenging now feel easy — but you can't articulate why they're easy.** If your fluency is AI-dependent, the struggle got absorbed, not resolved.
- **New team members aren't developing the skills you developed at their stage.** The pattern is generational — it shows up as a gap between how juniors learn now and how seniors learned then.
- **You're delegating sub-tasks that start with "figure out..." or "understand..."** These open-ended tasks are almost always developmental. The figuring out IS the learning. Don't absorb it.
- **Your workflows have no "struggle checkpoints."** If every step is optimized for speed and none for growth, absorption is happening by default.

The tell: **if you removed AI from your workflow, would you still know how to do the work — or would you need to learn from scratch?** If the answer leans toward "learn from scratch," absorption has been happening.

## The Absorption Check

When you decompose a task, add one question to your decomposition checklist:

> **Is this subtask developmental?** — meaning, does doing this yourself build expertise you need to maintain?

- **Developmental subtasks** → AI should scaffold, not solve. Use [[The Coaching Stance]]: let the AI guide, but keep the productive struggle. The AI can show you the path — you walk it.
- **Non-developmental subtasks** → delegate freely. If the task doesn't build any capability you need, don't waste your cognitive budget on it.
- **Unclear** → assume developmental. The cost of losing a developmental task is permanent (you never build the capability). The cost of doing a non-developmental task yourself is temporary (you spent time you could have saved). Err toward preservation.

## How the Pattern Closes — And Opens

The Absorption Pattern isn't inevitable. The same Informal Learning study that found 4.9% constructive engagement also found that scaffolded assistant support consistently enriches it. When AI is designed to guide rather than answer, constructive engagement rises.

The pattern closes through deliberate design:
- **Scaffold, don't solve.** For developmental tasks, the AI's job is to support your struggle, not end it.
- **Preserve the "figuring out."** The tasks that start with ambiguity — "figure out what's wrong," "understand why this works" — are the most developmental. Protect them most aggressively.
- **Build struggle checkpoints.** In every workflow, designate one step you do without AI — not because AI can't do it, but because you need to stay sharp. See [[The Coaching Stance]] for the practice.
- **Measure what matters.** If your metrics only track output volume, absorption is invisible. Add measures of capability maintenance.
