# The Sequencing Principle

## What It Is

**When you use AI matters more than whether you use it.** Think first. AI second. That's the whole idea.

The research across learning, creativity, and collaboration all points the same direction: the difference between AI that helps and AI that hurts is **the order you do things in.** Generate your own ideas before asking the AI for suggestions. Write your draft before using AI as editor. Have the meeting before reading the AI's summary.

This sounds obvious. It's not — because every interface defaults the other way. Open a blank chat window and the cursor blinks, waiting for you to ask something. The AI goes first by design. Breaking that default takes intention.

## Why It Matters for Moving Beyond Prompting

The operator mindset is reactive: you open AI, you type something, you see what comes back. The orchestrator mindset is proactive: you do your own thinking, then you hand off specific pieces for refinement.

Reversing the order changes everything:

- **You own the ideas, not the AI.** When you generate first, your perspective anchors the work. The AI's job is to expand and improve, not to originate.
- **You avoid the anchoring trap.** Once you see AI's suggestions, it's much harder to think outside them. Your brain gets stuck on what the AI said first.
- **You preserve your judgment muscles.** Every time you think first and then use AI to check or extend, you're practicing the skill. Every time you let AI go first, that skill goes unused.
- **You get better AI output.** When you arrive with a clear direction, you can give the AI specific, useful instructions instead of vague "help me with..." requests that yield generic results.

## The Three Domains

### Learning
Let the AI tutor you — explain, ask questions, guide — rather than give you the answer. The research shows that it's HOW you use AI that determines learning outcomes. A deliberate tutor prompt produces better results than a default "give me the answer" interaction. See [[The Coaching Stance]].

### Creativity
Always generate your own ideas first. Write them down — even rough ones. Then use AI to push them further: "combine ideas #3 and #7 in an extreme way," "give me 10 variations on #42." The AI is an amplifier, not a source. The anchoring effect means that AI's first suggestion crowds out your unique perspective. You can't un-see it.

### Writing
For many people, writing IS thinking. The act of drafting and redrafting develops ideas. If you let AI write your first draft, you skip the thinking. Ethan Mollick's practice: write the full draft alone, then ask AI to act as a reader ("was this unclear?") or editor ("give me 20 versions of this ending"). The AI polishes — you originate.

### Collaboration
Have the meeting first. Then use AI to summarize, find patterns, or surface what was missed. The AI as facilitator (spotting unheard voices, finding themes) works better than AI as replacement (everyone reads a summary instead of showing up).

## How to Spot It in Your Day

You're sequencing incorrectly when:
- You open a blank AI chat with no clear idea of your own
- You ask "what should I do about X?" before deciding what you think
- You read AI output before forming your own impression
- You can't separate what YOU contributed from what the AI suggested

You're sequencing correctly when:
- You have your own ideas, opinions, or draft BEFORE opening AI
- You give the AI specific, directive instructions based on your own thinking
- You can point to the parts that are yours vs. the AI's refinements
- You feel ownership over the final product, not just acceptance

## Try This

**The 2-Minute Think-First Rule**

Next time you reach for an AI tool:

1. Before opening it, take 2 minutes. Write down — on paper or in a note — your own answer to the question you were going to ask. Even if it's rough. Even if it's wrong.
2. NOW open the AI. Use it to check, extend, refine, or challenge your answer.
3. Notice: did the AI confirm your thinking? Contradict it? Add something you missed? You wouldn't know the answer to any of those questions if you'd asked first and thought later.

The 2 minutes of pre-thinking is the smallest unit of the Sequencing Principle. It's the difference between AI as a tool that sharpens your thinking and AI as a tool that replaces it.

## The Process Foundation — A Structural Parallel

There's an interesting echo at the organizational level. MIT Technology Review (July 2026) reports that companies with mature process frameworks (Lean, Six Sigma, BPM) get far more value from AI than companies that bolt AI onto shaky foundations. The same sequencing logic applies: **process first, AI second.** Structure before delegation. You can't hand off work to AI if you don't know what the work looks like — just like you can't ask AI for ideas if you haven't thought of any yourself.

The pattern scales from individual to organization: structure enables delegation at every level.
